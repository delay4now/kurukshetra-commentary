Below is a commentary on Episode 2 of the animated series *Kurukshetra: The Great War of Mahabharata* on Netflix.

## <u>New Characters Introduced in the Episode</u>

1\. **Yuyutsu** (@8:28): The son of King Dhṛtarāṣṭra, born to a maid rather than Queen Gāndhārī, and thus considered a half-brother of the Kauravas.

2\. **Dhṛtadyumna** (@17:11): The commander of the *Pāṇḍava* army and brother of Draupadi.

## <u>High Level Commentary</u>

In the second episode, the narrative shifts from observation to action, encompassing the establishment of combat rules, the transmission of the Bhagavad Gītā, and the first three days of the Kurukṣetra war. Before the clash begins, both sides agree to the codes of *Dharmayuddha* (righteous warfare), which dictate that combat must occur only between equals (charioteer against charioteer, foot soldier against foot soldier), hostilities must cease at sunset, and non-combatants must not be harmed.

As the armies face each other, Arjuna experiences a profound crisis. He drops his bow, *Gandiva*, paralyzed by the thought of slaughtering his kin and preceptors. While deeply relatable, this is a sudden contraction of his intellect into the purely individual and sentimental domain. He mistakenly places the temporal survival of his family above the maintenance of cosmic order. Krishna does not offer psychological comfort; instead, he delivers the Bhagavad Gītā, shifting Arjuna's perspective from the contingent to the Universal. This culminates in the revelation of the *Vishvarūpa* (Universal Cosmic Form), where Krishna displays the supra-individual reality, the divine omniscient form that encompasses all creation, time, and space, but ultimately embodying unity in all units of creation. Arjuna’s illusion of individual agency is shattered, and he learns that he is merely an instrument of a higher necessity.

With his intellect reoriented, Arjuna enters the fray. The first three days of the war depict the brutal initial friction between the opposing forces. Despite his renewed understanding, he initially fights Bhīṣma with a noticeable lack of intensity, still subconsciously tethered by reverence for his elder. This hesitation threatens the entire cosmic rectification, as Action (*karma*) cannot fulfill its purpose if diluted by sentimental attachment.

Seeing Arjuna’s debilitating restraint, Krishna violently breaks his own vow of non-combatance, lifting a chariot wheel as a weapon against Bhīṣma. This represents the ultimate crisis. Krishna’s vow marks a strict boundary defining his role as the *avatāra* and "Unmoved Mover". He provides knowledge and direction while the temporal friction of action must be executed by his human instrument (*kṣatriya*). By fighting Bhīṣma half-heartedly out of reverence, Arjuna’s so-called "compassion" reveals itself as supreme egoism. He elevates his sentimental attachments and psychological comfort above the maintenance of cosmic order, failing his *svadharma* (personal duties) and allowing the forces of *adharma* to gain ground.

By forcing Krishna to break his divine vow, Arjuna precipitates a cataclysm far greater than any physical slaughter. The Supreme Principle violating its own decreed laws, however, threatens total structural collapse of the manifested universe. Recognizing this absolute catastrophe, Arjuna throws himself at Krishna’s feet and annihilates his ego (as revealed in the beginning of the next episode). He accepts the temporal "sin" of kin-slaying, understanding it as insignificant compared to the subversion of cosmic equilibrium, and fully surrenders to his role as a ruthless, detached instrument of the Universal Will.

## <u>Miscellaneous Remarks and References</u>

**1\.** In the Kurukṣetra war, the armies are traditionally measured in a vast military formation known as an *Akṣauhini*, with the total mobilized force consisting of 18 *Akṣauhinis*: 11 for the Kaurava army and 7 for the Pāṇḍava army. A single *Akṣauhini* is traditionally described as comprising 21,870 chariots, 21,870 war elephants, 65,610 cavalry, and 109,350 infantry, reflecting the enormous magnitude of forces engaged in the epic battle.

**2\.** Krishna shows the *Vishvarūpa* to Arjuna during the delivery of the Bhagavad Gītā, precisely in the middle of the discourse. The Gītā can be divided into three key sections: The first ten chapters, begin with Arjuna's crisis and refusal to fight, during which Krishna dismantles Arjuna's emotional attachments and lays down the metaphysical principles of *Karma* (action), *Jñāna* (knowledge), and the immortality of the soul, preparing him intellectually for the ultimate revelation. Chapter 11, *Vishvarūpa Darśana Yoga* – the Yoga of the Vision of the Universal Form, presents the moment Arjuna explicitly asks to see Krishna's supreme nature and is granted the "divine eye" (*divya-drishti*) to behold this supra-sensory reality, revealing the *Vishvarūpa*. The final section (Chapters 12–18), follows Arjuna’s request to return Krishna to his familiar, human-like form after witnessing the terrifying totality of the *Vishvarūpa*, and Krishna spends the remaining chapters integrating this vision into practical guidance on devotion (*Bhakti*), the three *Gunas* (qualities of nature), and the final surrender of all actions to the Divine.

**3\.** Bhīṣma’s boon about death, often called his *Icchāmṛtyu* (death at will) was obtained from his father King Shantanu, that he could choose the moment of his death, making him invincible to natural death or ordinary circumstances and able to die only when he willed it himself. This is referenced in the show (@22:44), when he remarks to Krishna, “I would be lucky to be slain by you”.
